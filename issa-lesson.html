Japanese Literature · Lesson 29.2

Bashō Looks Out.
Issa Looks In.

Last lesson: Bashō turns a helmet and a cricket into a meditation on mortality. The world is the subject. Bashō is the witness.

Today: What happens when the poet becomes the subject?

一
Background

Kobayashi Issa (1763–1828)

OriginsSon of a farmer. Rural Japan — not the literary capitals.
LossLost his mother as an infant. Lost three children. Lost a wife.
StruggleSpent decades in legal disputes over his father's estate.
LegacyWrote over 20,000 haiku. More than any other major poet.

What might a life of grief and ordinary hardship do to a poet's eye?

二
The Distinction

Two Poets. Two Directions.

Bashō (1644–1694) Issa (1763–1828)
Gaze Outward — the world is the subject Inward and downward — the self is in the poem
Scale Battles, mountains, the season's turning Frogs. Children. Flies. Dreams.
Tone Austere, impersonal, transcendent Tender, comic, grief-inflected
Universality Seeks the universal in nature Finds the universal in the personal and the small
三
Before the Haiku

Read the Prose First

Turn to page 545 in your course reader. Read the excerpt from The Spring of My Life — Issa's diary entry about his infant daughter Sato.

Keep this in mind: you are reading a portrait of someone who is no longer alive when Issa writes the haiku.

Last summer, around the day for bamboo planting, a daughter was born to us in this world so full of sad events...

Once, when she saw a child her age with what is called a pinwheel, she wanted it for herself badly and fussed so much that we hurriedly got one for her. But soon she was chewing on it noisily and then she threw it away. Without showing a drop of regret, she turned her attention to something else, and began breaking the bowls that happened to be at hand. Soon bored with that too, she started tearing the thin paper off the sliding door. We said, "Well done! well done!" as though praising her. She thought we meant it and, cackling, went on tearing away intently. Not a speck of dust in her heart, she seemed as bright and pure as the full moon of autumn. As if witnessing a superb actor, I felt the wrinkles being smoothed out of my heart.

Again, when someone came along and said to her, "Where's the bow-wow?" she'd point at a dog, and when asked, "Where's the caw-caw?" she'd point at a crow. From her mouth to the tips of her fingers she brimmed with charm and lovableness...

— Kobayashi Issa, trans. Hiroaki Sato · p. 545

Now go back to page 544 and read the four Issa haiku.

四
Close Reading

The Prose Does Not Explain the Haiku. It Deepens It.

In a dream
my daughter lifts a melon
to her soft cheek.
— Kobayashi Issa · trans. Robert Hass · p. 544

Sato died at one year old. This haiku is not labelled. It gives no explanation.

Consider: What does the word dream do in line one? What would change if it were memory instead?

五
Close Reading

A Different Kind of Gap

A huge frog and I,
staring at each other,
neither of us moves.
— Kobayashi Issa · trans. Robert Hass · p. 544

Bashō

Would never put himself in the poem like this. The "I" does not appear. He is the camera, not the subject.

Issa

Puts himself in the same sentence as the frog. Eye-level with the smallest, most undignified creature.

What does that do?

Issa's world is smaller. That is the point. He is not standing before mountains or battlefields. He is watching a five-year-old count on his fingers. Dreaming of his dead daughter. The small is where his grief lives.

Unit Review · Japanese Literature

Where We Have Been

Three major texts. Three different answers to the question: what is the relationship between the self and the world?

The Pillow Book
Sei Shōnagon · c. 996 The self constructs itself through taste and attention. The list is not decoration — it is identity. What you notice defines who you are.
Zen Parables
Musō Soseki · Bankei · Shaseki-shū The self must be dissolved. Ego blocks wisdom. Virtue must be unconscious. Action is right only when it is not performed for virtue.
Haiku
Bashō · Buson · Issa The self witnesses (Bashō) or participates (Issa). Seventeen syllables carry a whole world — or a whole grief.
The Pillow Book — Key Concepts ▾

Zuihitsu ("following the brush"): a genre of fragmentary, associative writing. Not argument — accumulation.

Shōnagon's method: She constructs a persona through her preferences. The lists tell us what she values; the self is revealed through categories. Her lists are never neutral — they are social and aesthetic judgments.

Core question: How does attention shape social identity? The answer: it is social identity, for Shōnagon. To have taste is to have a self.

Exam note: Compare with Zen — where Shōnagon curates the self, the Zen masters want to erase it.

Zen Teachings & Parables — Key Concepts ▾

Three teachings (Musō Soseki): "Aim in Life" — anything gained can be lost; "Contamination of Virtue" — performed virtue isn't; "Hypocritical Scholars" — reading about wisdom isn't wisdom.

Two parables: "Publishing the Sutras" — right action requires the right motive; "Right and Wrong" — the ego-mind cannot adjudicate its own rightness.

The Zen paradox: The teaching that destroys the need for the teaching. Wisdom literature that makes wisdom literature unnecessary.

Exam note: "Publishing the Sutras" and "Right and Wrong" share a method — a challenge to received wisdom framed as narrative. Compare their techniques.

Haiku — Key Concepts ▾

Kigo (season word): haiku traditionally contains an implicit seasonal reference. The kigo situates the poem in the natural cycle even when its subject is personal.

The gap: Haiku is built on juxtaposition — two images placed in relation, with the connection left for the reader. The kireji (cutting word) signals the gap.

Bashō: The poet as detached witness. Universality through impersonality. The "I" is absent or implied.

Issa: The poet as participant. The "I" appears. Small creatures, private grief, domestic moments. Universality through intimacy.

Buson: The poet as painter. Visual precision, sensory detail, atmospheric distance. Less personal than Issa, less austere than Bashō.

Unit Review Quiz · Japanese Literature

Test Your Understanding

1. The Pillow Book belongs to a Japanese genre called:
A Haiku
B Zuihitsu ("following the brush")
C Mono no aware
D Satori literature
Correct. Zuihitsu means "following the brush" — fragmentary, associative writing that follows the writer's attention rather than a structured argument. It's the form Shōnagon essentially invented.
2. In the Zen parable "Contamination of Virtue," the master's point is that:
A Virtue requires daily practice to become habit
B Monks should avoid worldly contact to preserve their purity
C Virtue performed for recognition is not virtue at all
D The sutras contain all the wisdom a monk needs
Correct. Once a virtuous act is performed in order to be seen as virtuous, it becomes contaminated — it serves the ego, not the principle. This is the paradox at the heart of Zen ethics.
3. Which of the following best describes the structural difference between Bashō and Issa?
A Bashō uses longer poems; Issa stays strictly within 5-7-5
B Bashō removes himself from the poem; Issa places himself inside it
C Bashō focuses on grief; Issa focuses on nature's transcendence
D Issa uses kigo; Bashō avoids seasonal references
Correct. In "A huge frog and I" — the "I" appears directly. Bashō's poems rarely include the poet's presence so explicitly; he is the eye behind the image, not a subject within it.
4. Shōnagon's lists in The Pillow Book are primarily a tool for:
A Satirising the imperial court from a safe distance
B Documenting historical facts about Heian Japan
C Constructing and performing a social identity through aesthetic judgment
D Practising the Zen discipline of attention to ordinary things
Correct. The lists are not random — they reveal Shōnagon's hierarchy of value and taste, which is her identity at court. Her preferences are social acts, not private ones. This is the central argument around the core question: how does attention shape social identity?
5. The Issa haiku "In a dream / my daughter lifts a melon / to her soft cheek" gains its power primarily from:
A Its unusually strict adherence to the 5-7-5 syllable form
B The comic contrast between the melon and the infant
C What the reader brings to it from the prose portrait — Sato's death
D The kigo word "melon" which signals summer and life
Correct. Without the prose, this is a gentle dream image. Knowing Sato died at one year old transforms it: the dream is all that remains. The prose does not explain the haiku — it deepens it. This is why sequence matters.
6. Which of the following best captures the Zen paradox found across the parables?
A Wisdom must be earned through suffering and sacrifice
B The pursuit of wisdom through conscious effort may be the obstacle to wisdom
C Reading sacred texts is the surest path to enlightenment
D Wisdom can only be found in isolation from other people
Correct. "Hypocritical Scholars," "Contamination of Virtue," and "Publishing the Sutras" all dramatise the same trap: the ego turns the quest for wisdom into a performance. The trying is the problem. This is what makes Zen teaching so structurally interesting — it teaches the limits of teaching.
